# Drowned Harbor Visual Language

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** Preproduction visual direction
**Release stream:** P0.3
**Tale status:** Design-only
**Project names:** Terror Turn and The Underteller remain provisional

## 1. Visual north star

Drowned Harbor should look like a **drowned storybook diorama exposed at impossible low tide**.

The world combines:

- carved theatrical miniatures;
- hand-inked storybook contours;
- watercolor and gouache surface variation;
- oxidized brass, salt-stained timber, wet slate, clouded glass, and swollen paper;
- restrained cel shading rather than photoreal rendering;
- a board-game composition that remains readable from a living-room distance;
- lighting that feels authored by the lighthouse rather than by a neutral renderer.

The result should feel original, tactile, and illustrated—not like an unmodified low-poly, realistic-environment, or generic horror asset pack.

## 2. Core visual contradiction

The harbor is simultaneously:

- a navigable game board;
- a miniature theatrical set;
- a real supernatural place;
- a damaged illustrated memory.

Board readability always wins when atmosphere and legibility conflict.

The player should understand:

1. where each stable seat is;
2. which routes are open, flooded, damaged, or water-only;
3. what the current tide state is;
4. which mechanisms and objectives matter;
5. which information is public;
6. who may act next.

## 3. Rendering direction

### 3.1 Preferred approach

Use a stylized 2.5D or controlled 3D presentation with:

- strong silhouettes;
- simplified planes;
- two or three intentional value bands per material;
- selective ink-like edge treatment;
- hand-authored surface breakup;
- shallow atmospheric depth;
- restrained reflections;
- readable object exaggeration;
- localized animation rather than constant motion.

### 3.2 Avoid

- photorealism;
- generic low-poly pack appearance;
- uniformly clean geometry;
- excessive bloom;
- glossy plastic surfaces;
- noisy textures at television distance;
- blue-gray horror applied to every object;
- modern industrial-horror clichés;
- unreadably dark scenes;
- uncontrolled fog that hides interaction state;
- overly cute proportions that collapse the horror tone;
- direct imitation of a recognizable game, film, illustrator, or living artist.

## 4. Camera and composition

### 4.1 Shared-board camera

Preferred baseline:

- elevated three-quarter view;
- enough perspective to convey miniature depth;
- limited perspective distortion so routes remain readable;
- authored camera positions for major spaces;
- smooth bounded transitions between board regions;
- no essential information placed behind tall scenery.

### 4.2 Major compositions

Every major image or board state should have:

- one dominant vertical or directional anchor;
- a clear route hierarchy;
- one controlled warm-light source;
- dark negative space that frames rather than hides;
- a foreground, playable middle ground, and atmospheric background;
- visible tide evidence.

The lighthouse is normally the dominant vertical anchor.

## 5. Shape language

### Harbor and ordinary buildings

- low horizontal masses;
- sagging rooflines;
- irregular but readable rectangles;
- compressed doorways;
- timber frames that lean without becoming visually chaotic;
- broad silhouettes that support board-space recognition.

### Lighthouse

- narrow vertical authority;
- tapered tower;
- oversized lantern room;
- rotating beam represented as a clean directional wedge;
- cracked lens geometry that can be recognized in silhouette.

### Bellhouse

- squared base with a circular or arched upper mechanism;
- hanging vertical rope lines;
- a visible bell silhouette;
- repeated name-mark motifs.

### Harbor mechanisms

- circles, arcs, lenses, rings, seals, and indexed radial marks;
- brass and glass against dark timber;
- deliberate mechanical order within organic decay.

### Living characters

- upright, asymmetric silhouettes;
- one role-defining carried object or posture;
- readable seat marker separated from costume color;
- proportions grounded enough to preserve consequence.

### Restless forms

- interrupted silhouettes;
- missing or displaced weight;
- translucent or negative-space components;
- one unmistakable continuation-function motif.

### Tidebound forms

- curved, water-pulled silhouettes;
- layered memories expressed through doubled contours or secondary faces;
- retained evidence of the original character;
- transformation without generic zombie anatomy.

## 6. Material language

### Wet timber

- matte dark body;
- narrow reflected highlights;
- swollen seams;
- pale salt traces;
- carved or inked grain rather than photo texture.

### Oxidized brass

- muted green-brown body;
- selective warm edge exposure;
- used for bells, seals, lens housings, and ritual indexing.

### Harbor glass

- cloudy and imperfect;
- thick edge refraction;
- internal cracks shaped like routes or names;
- never clean modern glass.

### Paper and records

- swollen fibers;
- feathered ink;
- layered overwriting;
- red-brown or black indexing marks;
- readable text represented selectively rather than as dense noise.

### Water

- nearly black in deep spaces;
- green-gray in shallow exposed routes;
- restrained reflection;
- pale suspended memory shapes used sparingly;
- route state must remain distinguishable without color alone.

### Usage rule

Warm amber is scarce and meaningful. It identifies:

- active light;
- temporary safety;
- selected objectives;
- human intervention;
- choices that may guide something home.

Do not use amber as a general decoration color.

## 8. Tide-state visual grammar

Tide state must be visible through multiple channels.

### Lowest Tide

- exposed mud channels;
- pale salt edges;
- long grounded shadows;
- dry route markers;
- distant waterline.

### Returning Tide

- advancing reflective bands;
- wet route edges;
- moving debris;
- route markers beginning to distort;
- subtle vertical water indicators.

### Flooded Streets

- clear water-only connectors;
- partially submerged landmarks;
- boat and rope affordances;
- stronger lighthouse reflection;
- altered space silhouettes.

### Harbor Claim

- water entering upper structures;
- lamps rotating seaward;
- repeating memory figures;
- reduced safe negative space;
- stronger radial bell and lighthouse motifs.

### Final Submergence

- simplified final-stage composition;
- lighthouse, bell, archive, escape, and faction outcomes visually prioritized;
- ordinary exploration detail removed from attention.

## 9. Presentation profiles

The three profiles change intensity, not rules or information.

### Spooky

- softer silhouettes for drowned residents;
- implied bodily danger;
- brighter readable fog;
- storybook mystery emphasized;
- minimal explicit injury.

### Grim

- default contrast and decay;
- serious injury and transformation cues;
- emotional consequence;
- restrained body detail.

### Gore & Dread

- explicit wounds, waterlogged anatomy, and body-memory separation;
- stronger surface damage;
- no mechanical or route difference;
- no sexual violence;
- no constant gore that destroys visual hierarchy.

## 10. UI and icon language

UI should feel printed, carved, or indexed into the Tale without becoming decorative clutter.

Use:

- strong rectangular or arched panels;
- inked borders with controlled irregularity;
- brass index marks;
- paper, glass, and dark timber material cues;
- Roman or stable-seat numerals;
- clear text hierarchy;
- icons paired with labels for critical state.

Do not rely on:

- color alone;
- ornamental script for instructions;
- tiny distressed type;
- animated textures behind long text;
- horror effects that obscure ownership or choices.

## 13. Candidate image requirements

Every generated or externally created candidate must record:

- governed asset ID;
- generator, model, or creator;
- prompt and negative constraints;
- source references and rights;
- creation date;
- presentation profile;
- originality tier;
- intended use;
- human modifications;
- licensing status;
- approval state;
- replacement plan when not final.

Raw third-party restricted assets, receipts, keys, and proprietary source packages must not be placed in public GitHub.

## 14. Visual review checklist

A candidate passes preproduction review only when:

- the main silhouette is readable at thumbnail size;
- the scene reads as Drowned Harbor without a caption;
- it supports storybook horror rather than generic horror;
- interaction spaces remain legible;
- tide state is understandable through more than color;
- the lighthouse light has clear narrative authority;
- the image does not look like an unmodified marketplace pack;
- no protected character, recognizable brand, or artist imitation is present;
- source and generation provenance are complete;
- the candidate's next disposition is explicit.

## 15. Approval boundary

This document establishes preproduction direction only. It does not approve:

- final art style;
- production assets;
- storefront art;
- a final Underteller appearance;
- a Drowned Harbor production package;
- accessibility or television-readability claims;
- marketplace purchases;
- AI output as final art.
