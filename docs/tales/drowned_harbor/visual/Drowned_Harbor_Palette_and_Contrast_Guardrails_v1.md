# Drowned Harbor Palette and Contrast Guardrails

**Version:** 1.0
**Status:** P0.3 preproduction clarification
**Authority:** Supplements `Drowned_Harbor_Visual_Language_v1.md`
**Production approval:** None

## Owner direction

The preferred visual result may be dark, dreary, cold, and storm-pressured without becoming washed out, muddy, or monochromatic. The project should preserve the successful quality seen in the darker Gemini comparison: strong cool atmosphere, readable amber light, distinct silhouettes, and enough local color separation that the scene does not collapse into brown dirt, gray mud, or one undifferentiated dark mass.

## Core rule

**Darkness is an atmosphere and value structure, not a brown overlay.**

The Harbor should use a controlled cool maritime range as its dominant atmospheric family, while allowing materials and landmarks to retain distinct identities.

## Preferred dark-range behavior

Use:

- deep blue-green and slate shadows;
- muted cyan or tide-glass atmosphere in clouds, shallow water, and reflected light;
- black-green tidal mud rather than flat brown soil;
- verdigris brass and salt-pale edges to separate mechanisms from timber;
- restrained amber as the clearest warm directional accent;
- localized neutral paper, canvas, bone, and wet-stone values;
- enough midtone separation to preserve routes, travelers, props, and landmark silhouettes.

Do not use:

- global brown grading;
- sepia applied to the whole scene;
- identical dark values for timber, mud, slate, clothing, and water;
- cyan grading so strong that every material becomes blue;
- bright red as a competing universal accent;
- crushed blacks that remove route and character information;
- fog or bloom that erases material edges;
- desaturation that removes all local identity.

## Material separation targets

### Wet timber

Near-black brown or charcoal body, narrow cool reflections, pale salt seams, visible carved grain.

### Tidal mud

Black green-gray with shallow reflective pools, cracked matte islands, and cooler water channels. It must not read as ordinary dry dirt.

### Slate and stone

Cool blue-gray body with chipped pale edges and intermittent salt whitening.

### Oxidized brass

Muted verdigris body with selective exposed warm metal. It should remain legible against timber without becoming bright fantasy gold.

### Canvas and paper

Fog-paper, salt-bone, gray-green, and weathered neutral fabric. Small localized stains may exist, but canvas should not become another brown surface.

### Water and storm atmosphere

Deep tide-glass blue-green, clouded cyan-gray, and near-black reflective channels. Cool atmosphere may be pronounced in Grim scenes while material colors remain distinguishable.

### Human figures

Use small controlled local accents, varied neutrals, and readable value grouping. Stable-seat identity must never depend solely on costume color.

## Profile interpretation

### Spooky

- brighter fog-paper and slate midtones;
- clean ink contours;
- softer cool shadows;
- restrained danger detail;
- amber remains scarce but welcoming enough to guide attention.

### Grim

- deeper blue-green storm pressure;
- stronger amber-against-dark contrast;
- clear wet reflections;
- visibly cold and dreary without crushing midtones;
- no dominant brown or sepia cast.

### Gore & Dread

- retains Grim palette hierarchy;
- adds localized wound, rust, or body-memory color only where narratively justified;
- never floods the whole scene with red or brown;
- gore remains subordinate to route, seat, and objective readability.

## Reference hierarchy

For P0.3 and later visual work:

- `cgpt-v2.png` remains the provisional composition and world north star.
- `Gemini_2.png` remains the Spooky/readability reference.
- `Gemini_3.png` is elevated as the preferred **Grim atmosphere and cool-dark palette reference**.

The project should borrow Gemini 3's dark-but-readable cool atmosphere and amber contrast, while avoiding its overly global cyan cast and competing red market accent.

## Review test

A candidate fails palette review when:

- timber, mud, slate, water, clothing, and structures merge into one brown-gray mass;
- darkening removes the traveler route or landmark hierarchy;
- every shadow is the same hue;
- the scene appears clean but lifeless because all local color was removed;
- cyan, brown, red, or amber becomes a global wash instead of a controlled material or lighting choice.

A candidate passes preproduction palette review when:

- it feels cold, dreary, and dangerous;
- the lighthouse beam remains meaningful;
- each major material family reads differently;
- routes and travelers survive at reduced size;
- dark areas contain controlled detail rather than featureless black;
- the scene does not look uniformly brown, muddy, or washed out.

## Approval boundary

These guardrails establish preproduction palette intent only. They do not approve final color grading, HDR behavior, display calibration, accessibility, television readability, production textures, storefront art, or a Drowned Harbor production Tale.
