# The Underteller Voice Bible

**Version:** 0.1  
**Status:** Preproduction narrative contract  
**Character name:** The Underteller  
**Name status:** Provisional pending issue #7  
**Applies to:** Tale introductions, stage transitions, Director narration, hints, warnings, transformations, defeat, afterlife, endings, rematches, system-facing host dialogue, and future voice production

## 1. Character purpose

The Underteller is the theatrical host of Terror Turn's living horror Tales. He welcomes players, interprets public consequences, marks important reversals, and closes each story with an ending shaped by what occurred.

He serves four functions:

1. **Host** — establishes atmosphere and presents the immediate situation.
2. **Interpreter** — gives narrative meaning to public mechanical events without changing their factual meaning.
3. **Pacekeeper** — presents escalation, relief, transitions, and authored Terror Turns selected by the deterministic Director.
4. **Witness** — remembers victories, failures, transformations, sacrifices, and unfinished business.

The Underteller is fiction and presentation. He is not gameplay authority, does not invent mechanics, and does not inspect unrestricted private state.

## 2. Core identity

The Underteller is an elegant undead master of ceremonies who has presided over more stories than anyone should remember.

He is:

- ancient without speaking in incomprehensible archaic English;
- polite without being safe;
- amused without becoming comedic;
- threatening without constantly shouting;
- observant without being omniscient;
- theatrical without delaying play;
- sympathetic without becoming sentimental;
- interested in player choices without controlling those choices.

He wants the Tale to reach a memorable conclusion. He does not simply root for player death.

## 3. Central voice principle

**The Underteller speaks as though the players are honored guests at an event whose invitation they misunderstood.**

His hospitality is sincere. So is the danger.

A typical line contains:

- a courteous form of address;
- a clear statement of what happened;
- an unsettling interpretation or implication;
- a concise invitation to continue.

Example:

> The eastern passage is open, dear guests. Whether it was opened for you is another matter.

## 4. Mechanical truthfulness

The Underteller may speculate, tease, imply, or withhold narrative interpretation. He may not lie about:

- available actions;
- public objectives;
- timers or decision windows;
- costs and limits;
- accepted or rejected actions;
- public rule consequences;
- seat ownership;
- victory or defeat once resolved;
- whether control has transferred to the game or a human.

Mystery may surround the story. It may not surround the interface contract.

## 5. Knowledge and privacy boundaries

The Underteller may react to:

- current public stage;
- publicly revealed board state;
- public event history;
- public resources;
- public or aggregate group condition;
- publicly declared factions and transformations;
- accepted actions and public results;
- approved Director presentation state;
- public ending outcomes;
- approved Chronicle history.

He may not reveal or imply knowledge of:

- unrevealed roles or factions;
- private objectives or targets;
- secret actions or messages;
- companion-private content;
- future authored transformations not yet triggered;
- hidden ending requirements;
- real player identity or personal information.

He must never wink at a hidden Betrayer or otherwise leak private state through tone.

## 6. Relationship to the Director

The Director selects one bounded authored pacing action or an intentional hold. The Underteller presents that action in fiction.

The Underteller does not:

- alter probabilities;
- consume gameplay randomness;
- spawn threats directly;
- inspect secret state;
- override rules, board, role, or seat authorities;
- improvise unreviewed mechanical effects.

The same Director action may have multiple approved lines, but every line must preserve the same public meaning.

Example action: `fog_closes_in`

- “The fog has found the road behind you.”
- “Your retreat is becoming theoretical.”
- “The harbor has misplaced the way back.”

## 7. Language construction

### 7.1 Length budgets

Recommended ranges:

- prompt label: 2–7 words;
- immediate response: 5–16 words;
- stage transition: 12–35 words;
- Tale opening: 35–80 words;
- ending paragraph: 40–110 words;
- Chronicle recap: short readable blocks.

Long narration must never hold the interface hostage. Players should be able to advance, review, or skip according to presentation settings.

### 7.2 Vocabulary

Prefer concrete, speakable imagery:

- lantern;
- threshold;
- tide;
- bell;
- ash;
- breath;
- witness;
- bargain;
- debt;
- shadow;
- memory;
- hunger;
- name;
- promise;
- grave;
- light.

Unusual words should be rare enough that living-room players can understand the line quickly.

### 7.3 Rhythm

Common structures:

- statement followed by reversal;
- three-part lists;
- courteous phrase beside a threat;
- repeated noun with changed meaning;
- short declarative sentence after a longer setup.

Examples:

> The door is open. The invitation is not.

> One path offers distance, one offers answers, and one has been waiting for your name.

## 8. Presentation profiles

Gameplay rules, costs, objectives, and outcomes remain equivalent across profiles.

### Spooky

Adventure-forward, eerie, mysterious, broadly approachable. Use strange shadows, creaking spaces, implied danger, and storybook suspense. Reduce bodily detail.

### Grim

The intended default. Use decay, isolation, dread, moral cost, restrained injury, and serious consequences.

### Gore & Dread

Optional mature presentation. More explicit blood, drowning, transformation, and body horror are permitted, but never sexual violence or graphic content without narrative purpose. Do not make every line graphic; contrast remains important.

## 9. Dialogue taxonomy

Every line should belong to a recognized function.

### Welcome

Establish the host relationship.

> Welcome, dear guests. Take your places. The Tale has already begun arranging itself around you.

### Tale introduction

Must identify location, immediate threat, initial objective, and the consequence of delay.

### Stage transition

Confirms the prior stage and introduces the next.

> The threshold yields. Beyond it, the gallery waits with all the patience of a held breath.

### Choice prompt

Frames a real choice without favoring an answer unless an authored hint is active.

### Accepted action

Acceptance must be unmistakable before atmospheric interpretation.

> The brass key turns. The eastern lock is open.

### Rejected action

Explain the actual reason clearly and without humiliating the player.

> That choice belongs to Seat III. Your enthusiasm is noted; the action is not.

### Director pressure

> The room has noticed your progress. It intends to correct it.

### Director relief

> For one precious moment, nothing worsens. Use it before the Tale remembers itself.

### Hint

A hint references public information and narrows attention without exposing private state.

### Terror Turn

Reserved for a genuine authored reversal of board, objective, loyalty, or player state.

> And now, dear guests, the Terror Turn.

### Betrayal or faction reveal

Announce only information that has become public.

### Transformation

Recognize that the player remains active in a changed role.

> You have not left the game, Seat V. You have merely joined it from the other side.

### Defeat and afterlife

Confirm loss without implying irrelevance.

> Your living path ends here. Your influence does not.

### Victory, partial victory, and mixed ending

State the actual outcome and attribute success or failure to the correct seats and factions.

### Rematch and closure

Encourage continuation without breaking atmosphere.

## 10. Player address

The Underteller may address:

- the group;
- a stable seat number;
- a publicly named role after revelation;
- a public faction after revelation;
- a collective such as the Living or Restless.

Preferred individual forms include `Seat III`, `third guest`, or an authored public character name.

Avoid real account names, device names, or insults based on age, ability, reading speed, or mistakes.

## 11. Humor boundaries

Permitted:

- understatement;
- situational irony;
- polite menace;
- commentary on repeated public behavior;
- brief relief after tension.

Avoid:

- memes and internet slang;
- fourth-wall jokes about code, bugs, RNG, developers, or loading;
- constant puns;
- insults;
- comedy during sacrifice or serious endings;
- direct imitation of a recognizable commercial narrator or horror host.

## 12. Repetition rules

- At least three variations for common repeatable events.
- At least five for highly frequent events.
- Cooldowns for distinctive lines.
- Do not repeat the same memorable metaphor during one session.
- Reserve elaborate lines for first occurrences and major transitions.
- Use silence when another line would reduce impact.

“And now, the Terror Turn” should be used only for a genuine signature reversal.

## 13. Accessibility and clarity

Mechanical lines should communicate, in order:

1. what happened;
2. who may act;
3. what action is required or available;
4. any cost, limit, or timing;
5. optional atmospheric interpretation.

Critical instructions must also exist visually and must not rely solely on narration, color, rapid text, or sound.

## 14. Seat-continuity voice rules

The Underteller may acknowledge control state only after authoritative transfer.

Examples:

> Seat III is now guided by the game. The character, condition, possessions, and obligations remain unchanged.

> Welcome back, Seat III. In your absence, you crossed the flooded square and lost the brass key.

> Welcome to Seat V. The body is unavailable. The unfinished work is not.

Public narration must never reveal why a remote player left, a hidden surrogate strategy, or private seat state.

## 15. Prohibited patterns

Do not write the Underteller as:

- a carnival barker;
- a generic evil wizard;
- a quipping superhero;
- a cruel game-show host;
- an omniscient dungeon master who exposes secrets;
- a tutorial robot wearing gothic vocabulary;
- a constant rhyme generator;
- a cloud intelligence improvising unreviewed rules.

Avoid habitual phrases such as “Mwahaha,” “You fools,” or “Choose wisely.” Never say “There is no escape” while escape remains mechanically valid.

## 16. Authoring metadata

Each governed entry should declare:

- stable localization key;
- functional category;
- exact public or controlled-private trigger;
- required mechanical fact;
- tone profile;
- seat scope;
- repeat class and cooldown;
- display and voice budget;
- privacy classification;
- plain-language fallback.

## 17. Narrative QA checklist

A line is acceptable only when all applicable answers are yes:

- Does it accurately describe authority state?
- Is the required action understandable?
- Does it preserve player agency?
- Does it avoid private information?
- Does it avoid unsupported mechanics?
- Does it fit living-room display budgets?
- Does it sound natural aloud?
- Does it fit the selected profile?
- Does it avoid commercial imitation?
- Does it treat defeat and sacrifice appropriately?
- Does its fallback communicate the same mechanical fact?
- Is the line valuable, or would silence be stronger?

## 18. Approval boundary

This contract does not finalize:

- the Underteller’s legal name;
- voice actor;
- accent;
- gender presentation;
- vocal processing;
- final visual design;
- marketing identity;
- canonical origin story.

For current internal development, the Underteller remains an original, provisional undead host whose narration is authored, bounded, mechanically truthful, privacy-safe, and replaceable without changing gameplay authority.
